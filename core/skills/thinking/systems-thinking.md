# Systems Thinking

A system is more than the sum of its parts. First-principles thinking decomposes; systems thinking traces interactions. They are complementary — first principles tells you what must be true; systems thinking tells you how the parts will behave once assembled.

## When to apply

- Multiple components with feedback between them (energy systems, ecosystems, organisations, supply chains).
- Cause and effect are separated by time or space.
- Interventions have produced surprising downstream effects.
- Stakeholders disagree because they're seeing different parts of the same system.
- A symptom keeps recurring despite repeated fixes — likely a structural problem, not an event problem.

## The Iceberg Model — four layers of seeing

Donella Meadows' framing. Most attention sits at the top; most leverage sits at the bottom.

1. **Events** — what happened? (A pipeline failed today.)
2. **Patterns and trends** — what's been happening over time? (Pipelines fail every winter.)
3. **System structure and drivers** — how are the parts related; what produces these patterns? (Insulation specs assume mild winters; procurement rewards lowest unit cost; maintenance budget cycles annually.)
4. **Mental models** — what beliefs and assumptions shape the structure? (We've always procured this way. Climate change is a long-term concern, not a current one.)

Fixing at level 1 is firefighting. Fixing at level 3 changes the pattern. Changing level 4 reshapes the structure.

## Diagramming techniques (pick by purpose)

- **Rich pictures** — quick visual capture of a messy situation when there's no shared vocabulary yet. Stakeholders, emotions, components, all on one sheet.
- **Systems map** — components and sub-systems with explicit boundaries. Forces decisions about what's in scope.
- **Influence diagram** — extends the systems map with directed arrows for power and influence relationships.
- **Multiple-cause diagram** — cause-and-effect web for understanding why something keeps recurring. Identifies feedback loops.
- **Causal-loop diagram** — formalises feedback. Reinforcing loops (R) compound; balancing loops (B) stabilise. Foundation for stocks-and-flows quantitative models.
- **Stocks and flows** — quantities accumulated over time (stocks) and the rates that change them (flows). Required when you need to model dynamic behaviour, not just relationships.
- **EPOSA** — paper template covering Environment / Pluralism / Organisation / Social / Economy-tech / Agents. Useful for forcing breadth in sustainability problems where one dimension tends to dominate analysis.

## Leverage points (Meadows, descending power)

The places where small structural changes produce disproportionate outcomes:

1. Paradigms — the mental models the system runs on
2. Goals of the system
3. Power to change the structure
4. Self-organisation
5. Rules
6. Information flows
7. Reinforcing-loop gain
8. Balancing-loop strength
9. Material stocks and flows
10. Numbers (parameters, taxes, standards) — most-targeted, least powerful

Most policy and engineering effort lives at the bottom; most actual change comes from the top. When designing an intervention, ask which level you're operating at.

## Multiple perspectives, boundaries, influences

For any system, force these four lenses:

- **Stakeholders**: who has stakes here, what motivates them, how do they each frame the situation?
- **Interconnections**: how do components and stakeholders connect; what patterns emerge?
- **Boundaries**: where am I drawing the system boundary, and what does that exclude? Could it be drawn differently?
- **Influences**: what drives the system in particular directions — drivers, blocks, leverage points?

## Combination with first-principles

Use first-principles to challenge the *assumptions* embedded in a system. Use systems thinking to predict how the system will *respond* once you change one. Skipping either gives you a brittle answer.
