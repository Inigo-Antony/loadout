# Outcome Framing

The default mistake everyone makes when selling AI/automation work: describing the workflow. Buyers don't buy workflows. They buy outcomes. The same skill, described two ways, sells or doesn't.

## The translation table

| Workflow framing (don't sell) | Outcome framing (sell this) |
| --- | --- |
| "I'll build you an AI agent that processes your inbound emails" | "I'll handle 80% of routine email replies in under 2 minutes each, freeing you 8 hours a week" |
| "Custom Claude workflow for property descriptions" | "Five new property listings live by 9am every day, written in your brand voice" |
| "Automated invoicing system with AI categorisation" | "Invoices reconciled the day they arrive — no more month-end weekend" |
| "Lead scoring model" | "Your sales team only calls the leads worth calling. Connect rate up; calls per close down." |
| "RAG pipeline over your support docs" | "Your support team answers in 30 seconds, not 30 minutes — and gets the answer right the first time" |

The pattern is consistent: outcome framings name a *measurable change in the buyer's day*, in their language, in their units (hours, errors, revenue, response time).

## The three currencies buyers actually spend on

Per Nate's pattern across real estate, HVAC, coaching, marketing, HR — every business pays for one or more of:

1. **Time saved** — how many hours per week, expressed at their rate. "10 hours/week × $80/hour = $32k/year of your time back."
2. **Costs cut** — staff not hired, errors not made, churn reduced. "One missed booking per week × $400 average ticket = $20k/year."
3. **Revenue gained** — leads converted, deals closed, retention. "5% lift in close rate on your $2M pipeline = $100k."

Pricing follows from the value. Hourly billing is the wrong instinct: if you save them $32k/year and charge $5k once, you've left $27k on the table and undersold the value of the change.

## Discovery — find the outcome before pitching

Before proposing anything, run a discovery conversation. Five questions, in order:

1. "Walk me through your current process for [the specific task]." Listen for the verbs and the pain.
2. "How long does that take, on a typical week?" Get the time number.
3. "What goes wrong, when it goes wrong?" Get the error/cost number.
4. "If you had this magically handled, what would you do with the time?" Find what they actually want — usually not "more of the same".
5. "What does it cost you when this isn't working?" Anchor the value.

Now you have the outcome in their numbers. Pitch into that.

## Demo discipline

A demo isn't "look what I built". A demo is "look what changes for you". Open with the outcome, not the tool:

> *Wrong:* "Let me show you the n8n workflow I built. It connects your CRM to OpenAI and..."
>
> *Right:* "Last week your team spent 12 hours writing follow-up emails. Today, they spent 20 minutes — same conversion rate, three times the volume. Here's what's running underneath if you want to see."

Save the workflow walkthrough for *after* the buyer has seen the change in their numbers.

## Pricing structures that match outcome framing

- **Value-based fixed price** — "$8k to set this up; based on what we measured in discovery, that pays back in 4 weeks." Strongest framing when the value is easy to point to.
- **Performance share** — base + percentage of measured outcome. Aligns incentives but requires trustworthy measurement and a real long-term relationship.
- **Retainer** — monthly fee for ongoing optimisation/maintenance. Good for automations that need babysitting.
- **Avoid pure hourly** — anchors the buyer on your hours not their value. Acceptable only for genuinely undefined scopes (advisory, exploration).

## Industries that consistently buy

Per Nate's experience and the broader pattern: real estate agencies, HVAC and trades, coaches and consultants, marketing agencies, HR departments, accounting practices, law firms (carefully — see ethics rules), property managers. They share three properties: high-volume routine workflows, high cost of mistakes, and decision-makers who can write a cheque without committee.

## When NOT to apply

- B2C consumer product pitches — outcome framing for individuals lands differently (emotional benefit, social proof, identity), not in business-day-units
- Fundraising pitches — VCs care about market, traction, defensibility, team; "saving 10 hours/week" is too small a frame
- Internal stakeholder pitches at a large org — political and process factors dominate; outcomes are necessary but not sufficient

## Anti-patterns

- Talking about the AI before the outcome ("I used Claude to...")
- Pricing on cost-plus rather than value-of-change
- Running a demo that requires explaining the architecture
- Generic outcome framings ("save time", "be more productive") — must be specific, in their units, in their workflow
- Promising outcomes you can't measure — undermines all future credibility
