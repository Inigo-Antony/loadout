# SEO & Marketing

Marketing is distribution. Without distribution, the best content / product / service goes unseen. Most creators skip distribution because creating is fun and marketing feels like a chore. The market doesn't care about effort; it cares about reach.

## Core principle: own your audience

Every marketing decision splits into "owned" or "rented" channels.

- **Owned** — email list, your website, a community you run. You control the relationship.
- **Rented** — social platforms, SEO traffic, paid ads. The platform owns the relationship; their algorithm change is your business risk.

Use rented channels to drive traffic to owned channels. Every interaction should aim to capture the email or membership.

## SEO — what still works

Search has changed. Google's AI overviews, ChatGPT/Perplexity/Claude as primary research tools — the old "rank #1 and done" model has cracked. What still works:

- **Specific intent matching.** Targeting "best CRM" is impossibly competitive. Targeting "best CRM for solo financial planners under $100/month" matches a real intent with low competition.
- **Comprehensive coverage of a narrow topic.** Become *the* resource for the narrow topic; broader topics will follow as the domain authority builds.
- **First-hand experience signals.** Google's E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) increasingly rewards content that's clearly from someone who's done the thing, not someone who's read about it.
- **Speed and Core Web Vitals.** Lighthouse score ≥ 90 mobile is table stakes.
- **Internal linking.** New posts link to related older posts; old posts get updated to link to new ones. Pages don't rank in isolation.

What doesn't work in 2026:
- Keyword stuffing (Google's been past this for a decade)
- Thin AI-generated content at scale (penalised; user behaviour signals identify it)
- Backlink schemes (penalised; rarely worth the risk)
- Over-optimised "perfect SEO" content (reads as inauthentic; users bounce; rankings fall)

## GEO — optimising for AI search

The new layer: when someone asks Claude/ChatGPT/Perplexity, will your content be cited? GEO (Generative Engine Optimisation) is the discipline.

What matters for AI citation:
- **Structured, scannable content.** AI parses headers, lists, tables. Walls of prose get summarised away.
- **Clear declarative statements.** AI can cite "X is Y because Z" easily; vague hedging gets dropped.
- **Authoritative sources cited inline.** AI follows your citations; well-sourced content earns trust signals.
- **Schema markup** (FAQ, HowTo, Article schema) — explicit metadata helps AI extractors.
- **Listed in AI-trusted sources.** Wikipedia, established publications, GitHub stars, academic citations all influence which sources AI weights.

Practical: write for both. Humans prefer narrative; AI prefers structure. Pick a hybrid: opening narrative + structured key-points + sourced specifics.

## Channel selection

Pick channels by audience, not by your preference. The right channels for B2B SaaS founders are different from indie hackers from energy-sector professionals.

| Goal | Best channels |
| --- | --- |
| Service-business clients | LinkedIn, direct outreach, referrals, niche communities |
| Digital products | Twitter/X, YouTube, niche-forum SEO, paid ads |
| SaaS sign-ups | Content SEO, Product Hunt, paid ads, integrations as channels |
| Local services | Google Business Profile, local SEO, referrals, Facebook groups |
| Audience building | Newsletter + one social channel + one long-form |
| Course/community | Email list (always), webinars, partner promotion, affiliate |

Don't try every channel. One channel done well beats five channels done poorly.

## Email list — the long-term asset

The single asset that compounds across every other channel and isn't subject to platform risk:

- **Capture every interaction.** Free download, course, newsletter signup, content unlock — every visitor who's interested gets an offer to subscribe.
- **Send consistently.** Weekly or fortnightly is the sustainable cadence for most. Less than monthly, the list goes cold.
- **Provide value first; sell occasionally.** A list trained on free value reads sales emails; a list trained on sales emails unsubscribes.
- **Segment when it matters.** Customers vs prospects, free vs paid, by interest area. Don't over-segment early.
- **Own the platform.** Self-hosted (Listmonk), or independent providers (ConvertKit, Buttondown). Don't put your list on a platform that conflates email + course + community + payment — it locks you in.

## Paid ads — when it makes sense

Paid ads are an amplifier, not a starter. They amplify what's working organically.

- Don't run ads to a landing page you haven't tested with organic traffic
- Don't run ads to a product that hasn't sold to existing audience
- Start with retargeting (cheaper, higher converting) before prospecting
- Cost per acquisition must clear lifetime value with margin — measure both before scaling
- Stop ads when they stop performing; sunk-cost reasoning here burns money daily

## The 90-day discipline

For any new marketing initiative, commit to 90 days and one channel before evaluating. Most efforts fail because they're measured at week 3 and abandoned for the next shiny channel. 90 days isn't long; it's the minimum to see whether the approach works.

If you can't commit 90 days, don't start. Pick a smaller initiative.

## Metrics that matter (vs vanity)

| Vanity | Real |
| --- | --- |
| Followers | Email list size |
| Likes | Comments / replies / DMs |
| Page views | Time on page; conversion rate |
| Total subscribers | Active subscribers (engaged in last 90 days) |
| Articles published | Articles indexed and ranking |
| Demos booked | Demos converted to engagement |

Track 2–3 real metrics. Reporting on 12 metrics is reporting on none.

## When NOT to apply

- Service business with referral-driven growth — distribution is happening through clients, not marketing
- Pre-product / pre-validation — fix the product first, marketing won't save a bad product
- Hyper-specialist B2B with <100 buyers in the world — direct outreach beats marketing every time

## Anti-patterns

- Posting daily across five platforms with no measurement
- "Content strategy" with no email capture
- Optimising for the algorithm at the expense of the audience
- Running ads to cold traffic to a generic landing page
- Comparing your channel-3 results to someone else's channel-30 results
- Treating SEO as a one-time project rather than a sustained practice
- Building on rented platforms only
