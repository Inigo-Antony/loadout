# Outreach & Applications

The discipline that makes the difference between cold outreach that gets read and cold outreach that gets deleted. Same principles apply across job applications, sales outreach, partnership proposals, podcast pitches, and grant applications — adapted to the specific format.

## The universal structure

Every effective cold message has the same shape:

1. **Hook** — a specific reason you're writing to *this* recipient, not a generic broadcast. One sentence.
2. **Context** — who you are, with the credential most relevant to them. One sentence.
3. **Value or ask** — what you're offering or what you want. Specific and measurable.
4. **Close** — concrete next step. Specific and small.

Three to five sentences total for most outreach. Anything longer is being read for politeness, not interest.

## What makes the hook work

- Reference something specific: a paper they wrote, a project they ran, a comment they made, a recent outcome at their company. Generic compliments ("I love your work") are detected instantly as templated.
- Show you've done five minutes of research. The minimum bar is higher now that everyone uses AI to draft outreach; specifics still beat templates.
- Don't open with "I hope this finds you well". It's filler that delays the point.

## The credential paragraph — pick one anchor

You have many credentials. The cold message has space for one. Pick the one most relevant to *this recipient's interests*, not the one most relevant to you. A first-author publication is the right anchor for a researcher; a working production system is the right anchor for an engineering manager; a measurable client outcome is the right anchor for a business owner.

State the credential as a fact, not a claim. "First-author paper in *Journal of Energy Storage* on zinc-ion batteries" — not "I've done extensive research in batteries".

## Job applications — the specifics

Cover letters follow the same shape. 250–350 words; under 400 always.

- **Open with the strongest fact**, not "Dear Hiring Manager, I am writing to apply..."
- **Address the keystone requirements** of the posting in the body — not all requirements; the 2–3 that gate the role.
- **Include hard-filter responses up front**: visa status, location, language requirement. If they need C2 German and you don't have it, flag it; don't waste their time and yours hiding it.
- **Frame gaps honestly**: "I haven't worked with X, but have used Y for similar purposes and would expect a short ramp" — stronger than dodging.
- **Close with concrete availability**, not "I look forward to your response".

CVs follow standard reverse-chronological. One page unless the role legitimately rewards more. Each role: one line of context, 2–4 bullets of *outcomes* (not duties) with numbers where possible. Publications and projects fully formatted in their sections.

## LinkedIn / InMail outreach

3–5 sentences. The format is unforgiving — long messages get scrolled past. Lead with a real connection or specific fact about the recipient. Sign off with one credential line + visa status if relevant.

The ask is concrete. "Could we have a 15-minute call about [specific topic]?" — not "would love to learn from your experience".

## Sales outreach

Different from job apps in that the recipient has a budget, not a process. The same shape, but:
- Lead with the *outcome* you'd produce, not the service you sell (see `business/outcome-framing.md`)
- Reference a specific situation suggesting they have the problem you solve
- Ask for a small commitment first (15 minutes, a brief reply, an introduction) — not a meeting

## Application questions ("Why us?", "Tell us about a challenge")

- Lead with the *non-obvious* answer; generic ones get filtered. "Why us?" answered with the company's mission statement is a generic answer.
- Treat each question as a small essay: claim → evidence → implication.
- Word counts are real. 200 words means 180–220, not 400. Going long signals you don't read instructions.
- Specific stories beat abstract qualities. "Hardworking" is a claim; "shipped X under Y constraint with Z outcome" is a demonstration.

## Pitches — podcast, conference, partnership

A pitch is outreach with a deliverable attached. The deliverable proves you've done the work and decreases their effort.

- **Podcast:** 3–5 episode topic ideas you'd be a strong guest for, each one specific to *their* audience, each with a one-line summary of what listeners would learn.
- **Conference:** abstract of the talk, audience takeaways, why this audience specifically.
- **Partnership:** the value flowing each direction, made concrete; not "let's collaborate" but "I can do A, you can do B, the result is C".

## Honest framing principles

The cover letter rules generalise to all outreach:

- Don't restate their material back to them ("As your job posting describes...")
- Don't open with effusive praise — it reads as buttering up
- Don't list every relevant project; pick one and go deep
- Don't promise outcomes you can't deliver — undermines all credibility forever
- Don't fabricate a connection — references that don't survive verification end the relationship faster than no reference at all

## Volume vs personalisation

The decision people get wrong: sending 100 templated messages and getting nothing, vs sending 10 personalised messages and getting two. The math is identical for hours invested; the second produces results.

Volume is rarely the answer. If you need volume, narrow your targeting until each message is sufficiently personalised at a viable cadence.

## When NOT to apply

- The recipient is clearly oversubscribed (extensive recent press, big platforms) — your message will be one of hundreds; lower base rate
- You don't have a real reason for them to engage — fix the value before sending
- You're applying for roles you don't actually want — wastes both sides' time

## Anti-patterns

- Templated openings detectable across recipients
- "I am passionate about" / "I am writing to apply" / "I came across your..." / "I hope this email finds you well"
- Burying the ask in paragraph three
- Listing every credential when one would land harder
- Hiding gaps that will surface in the interview
- Mass outreach with no follow-up plan; one message gets ignored, three over weeks gets read
