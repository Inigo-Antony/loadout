# Digital Products

The category covers anything you create once and sell repeatedly — templates, planners, guides, ebooks, prompt packs, courses, paid newsletters, downloadable software. Different from service work in three ways: (1) creation is upfront, revenue is later; (2) marketing is the actual job, not creation; (3) most products earn near zero, a few earn well, the distribution is heavy-tailed.

## The 80/20 reality

Per the consistent pattern across every credible source: **creating the product is 20% of the work; marketing is 80%.** The default failure mode is inverting this — creators spend three months perfecting a product, launch to silence, and conclude "the product wasn't good enough" when the real issue was zero distribution.

Plan accordingly. If you're not willing to spend more time selling than building, build a service instead.

## Validate before building

The cost of validation is hours; the cost of building unvalidated is weeks-to-months. Validate first.

**Cheap validation methods:**

1. **Pre-orders / paid waitlist** — landing page describes the product, takes payment or refundable deposit. Conversion rate to actual purchase is the signal. If 20 people land and zero pay, the product won't sell when built.
2. **Paid pilot** — sell the manual version (you delivering it personally) to 2–3 people first. If they pay for the bespoke version, the productised version probably has a market.
3. **Audience signal** — post the idea where your audience already is. Direct messages saying "I'd buy that" are the strongest signal. Likes are not.
4. **Competitor analysis** — products in this category already exist? Look at their reviews. Are people actively complaining about the same gap your product fills? Existing market with unmet need is the best signal.

If you can't validate, the product is too vague. Specific products validate; "a productivity course" doesn't.

## Categories and what works

| Category | Effort to create | Marketing intensity | Typical price | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Notion/Airtable templates | Low (days) | High | $19–$79 | Saturated; needs niche or distribution |
| Prompt packs | Low (days) | High | $9–$49 | Increasingly saturated; specialisation matters |
| Ebooks / guides | Medium (weeks) | Very high | $9–$49 | Most undersell; bundle with audience or course |
| Cohort-based courses | High (weeks-months) | Medium | $200–$2000 | Premium pricing, premium effort, premium outcomes |
| Self-paced courses | Very high (months) | High | $50–$500 | High completion rates require structure not just video |
| Paid newsletters / Substack | Continuous | Built-in | $5–$15/mo | Compounds with audience; slow start |
| Downloadable software | High (weeks-months) | Medium | one-time or sub | Closer to SaaS economics; consider that path |

## Pricing

- **Anchor on value, not effort.** A 30-page ebook that solves a $5k problem is worth more than the page count suggests.
- **Tiers signal seriousness.** Single-tier $19 reads as low-effort. $19/$49/$99 with differentiated content reads as a real offering.
- **Avoid race-to-the-bottom price points.** $5 products attract bargain hunters who complain the most and refer the least.
- **Test prices.** First 50 buyers at $39, next 50 at $79, observe conversion. The "right price" is the one that maximises revenue, not units.

## Distribution channels

- **Owned audience** (newsletter, social, community) — best margins, slowest to build, compounds
- **Marketplace** (Gumroad, Etsy, AppSumo, ProductHunt) — fast distribution, lower margin, less control
- **Direct sales / outreach** — highest margin, doesn't scale, useful for high-ticket
- **Affiliate / partner channels** — works once you've established the product and have margin to share

Do not build only on rented platforms. Marketplace algorithms change; your audience belongs to them, not you. Capture email at every interaction so you have a list outside any platform.

## The minimum viable product (MVP) discipline

First version is the smallest that delivers the outcome. Cut everything that isn't load-bearing for the buyer's first win. Examples:

- Ebook: 30 pages, one core insight, three concrete examples. Not 200 pages of comprehensive coverage.
- Course: one module that produces one tangible outcome. Not the full curriculum.
- Software tool: one workflow done well. Not a feature-comparable competitor.

You'll add more after launch based on what buyers actually ask for. Building the comprehensive version first is how products miss launch windows.

## Refunds, support, ethics

- Honour refunds. The 1–3% who refund cost less than the reputation damage of fighting them.
- Respond to support questions within 24 hours. Buyers remember silence longer than fixes.
- If the product is AI-assisted in creation, that's fine; if the product is "100 prompts" but they're generic, that's the problem (quality, not provenance).
- Don't sell the same product as five different "courses" with different titles. The audience overlaps.

## When the product should become something else

If buyers consistently ask for customisation or implementation help, that's a service-business signal — productise less, sell setup more. If buyers consistently use the product daily and would pay monthly, that's a SaaS signal — turn it into hosted software with subscription. Listen to what they're trying to buy that you're not selling.

## When NOT to apply

- You don't have audience and don't enjoy marketing — products are wrong; services are right
- The "product" is a thinly disguised version of one client's deliverable — likely too narrow
- The market is saturated and you have no differentiator — pick a niche or pick a different product
- Validation didn't validate — building anyway is sunk-cost reasoning before any cost is sunk

## Anti-patterns

- Spending three months building before talking to buyers
- Pricing low because "I'm new" — depresses revenue and signals low quality
- Launching to no one and treating sales as a marketing failure rather than a distribution failure
- Building features instead of marketing existing capability
- Treating the product as the business — it's only the deliverable; the business is the marketing
