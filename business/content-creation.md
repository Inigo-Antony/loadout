# Content Creation

Content is downstream of three things: a clear point of view, a repeatable production process, and distribution. Most creators have one of three; sustained creators have all three.

## Point of view

Generic content is invisible. The first thing to nail is what specifically you have to say that others aren't.

- **Position derives from intersection.** "Marketing advice" is generic. "Marketing for engineering-led B2B SaaS startups under 50 people" is positioned. Two narrowings (who + context) does most of the work.
- **Strong opinions, defensible.** A take readers can disagree with sticks. A take everyone already agrees with is wallpaper. Consensus content is safe and forgettable.
- **Specificity over comprehensiveness.** One concrete example, fully developed, beats five abstract bullet points.

If you don't know your point of view, content production won't fix it. Step away from production until you can write your position in one sentence.

## The production pipeline

```
idea → outline → draft → edit → ship → measure → repurpose
```

Each stage is a different skill; doing them simultaneously is why writers struggle.

- **Idea capture** — running list, captured as they happen. Don't try to invent ideas at the desk; capture them when they occur (in conversation, while reading, while running) and decide which to develop later.
- **Outline before drafting.** Twenty minutes on outline saves an hour on draft. The outline is where the argument is built; drafting just renders the argument into sentences.
- **Draft fast, edit slow.** Get the words out without judging them. Edit on a different sitting if possible — your editing self is more discerning than your drafting self.
- **Edit for: structure first, then sentence-level, then proofreading.** In that order. There's no point fixing commas in a paragraph that's getting cut.
- **Ship at "good enough", not "perfect".** Perfect doesn't ship; "good enough and a real audience response" does.

## AI assistance — what works, what doesn't

Claude is genuinely useful for: outlining (decompose a topic into structure), unblocking (rough draft to react against), research compression (summarise sources), repurposing (article → tweet thread → script).

Claude is not useful for: replacing the point of view, generating finished prose that sounds like you, deciding what to write about. Output that goes through Claude unedited has a recognisable AI shape — generic openings, padded transitions, hedged conclusions, "in conclusion" closures, em-dash overuse, balanced "on the other hand" structures. Audiences recognise it; trust drops fast.

If you use AI assistance:
- Generate to a brief, not a topic. Specific brief in → specific draft out.
- Edit heavily. The first 1000 words of your unedited Claude output are not what you want associated with your name.
- Bring your voice in the rewrite. Use specific words you actually say, examples from your actual life, opinions Claude wouldn't risk.
- Don't disclose unless asked or the platform requires it; do disclose if the contract requires; never lie if directly asked.

## Format-specific tactics

**Long-form articles (1,500+ words):**
- One thesis. State it in the first paragraph.
- Section headers that summarise their section's argument, not topic labels.
- Concrete examples in every major section.
- Close with implication, not summary.

**Video scripts:**
- First 10 seconds is the entire pitch — what you'll say, why they should care.
- Spoken cadence ≠ written cadence. Read it aloud while writing.
- Visuals planned in the script, not after.

**Social posts (X, LinkedIn, Threads):**
- One idea per post.
- Strong hook = first line.
- Replies/comments are the actual content; posts are the bait.
- Don't post and ghost — engage in your own thread for the first hour.

**Newsletters:**
- Consistent cadence > clever timing. Same day, same time, every send.
- Three sections rather than one long; readers scan, not read.
- The cold-open hook beats the "hi everyone" intro every time.

## Repurposing

One piece becomes many. The cost of repurposing is small; the audience cost of channel-monogamy is large.

```
long-form article
  → Twitter/X thread (the argument as 8 tweets)
  → LinkedIn post (the conclusion + a question)
  → newsletter (the article + your reflection)
  → YouTube/short video (the punchline + a story)
  → podcast segment (the topic + a guest)
```

Don't post the same thing across all channels in identical form — adapt to the channel's grammar. Same idea, different shape.

## Measurement and iteration

Track:
- **Reach** — what got seen
- **Engagement** — comments, replies, time-on-page (engagement is signal of resonance, not popularity)
- **Conversion** — what got people from passive audience to email list, paid product, or client

Reach without conversion is vanity; conversion without reach is too small to matter. Both, eventually.

After every piece, a 5-minute review: what worked, what didn't, what would I do differently. Don't analyse to paralysis; just notice the pattern.

## Production cadence

The hardest skill in content is consistency. Defaults that work:

- **One core piece per week** (long-form article, or substantial newsletter, or YouTube video). Less than this isn't a content practice.
- **Daily presence on at least one social channel** — not for the platform's sake, but to stay in the conversation.
- **Content calendar planned 4–8 weeks ahead** — beats the "what should I post today" panic.

If you can't sustain weekly, sustain biweekly. Quarterly bursts followed by silence don't build audience.

## Anti-patterns

- AI-generated content posted unedited
- "Engagement bait" that doesn't deliver on the implied promise
- Trend-chasing topics outside your stated POV
- Long preambles before the actual point
- Closing with "let me know what you think" — the substance should provoke comment without prompting
- Treating distribution as someone else's problem
- Cross-posting identical text everywhere (algorithms penalise; audiences notice)

## When NOT to apply

- You're a service provider with a small fixed client base — referral-based growth dominates content; spending hours on social pays poorly
- The audience you want isn't on social media (some B2B niches, regulated industries, geographic markets)
- You don't have the operational margin to sustain — three months of inconsistency hurts more than three months of nothing
